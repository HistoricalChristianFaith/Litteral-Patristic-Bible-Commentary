1:20

 
 
 
 
The mystery of the seven stars, which thou sawest in my right hand, and the seven golden candlesticks. The seven stars are the angels of the seven churches. And the seven candlesticks are the seven churches.
 
 
 
 
St. Gregory Nazianzen
 
And to the presiding Angels, for I believe, as John tells me in his Revelation, each Church has a guardian. (Orations)
____________________________________________________________
 
St. Andrew of Caesarea
 
Since Christ is the true light, because of this, those abundant with his light are lamps as they shine in the night of this present life. Naturally, the churches are called lampstands, which as the luminaries have the word of life according to the Apostle. The lamps and lampstands are gold due to the honor and purity of the faith in them. An angel has stood guard for each of these churches, just as the Lord says. And, Gregory the Theologian had understood the present chapter: he figuratively called them "stars" due to the brightness and clarity of their nature.

____________________________________________________________

Apringius of Beja
 
The stars placed in the right hand of God are the souls of the saints, or, what is the same thing, the entire congregation of the blessed who have been and who will be until the consummation of the world. In a similar way, we have said that the 7 lampstands are the one true Church that has been established during the 7-day period of the world, which is founded by faith in the Trinity and which is made strong by the sacrament of the Heavenly mystery.

St. Bede
 
stars. That is, the rulers of the Church. For the priest, as Malachi says, is "the angel of the Lord of hosts.(Mal 2:7)"

St. Beatus of Liébana
 
‘And then, He unfolds the vision that He has shown:’

“The mystery of the seven stars which thou saw in my right hand, and the seven golden candlesticks. The seven stars are the seven angels of the seven churches.”

‘Here is what we named a little above: the stars set in the right hand of God to be the souls of the Saints, or all the congregation of the Saints and the Blessed as well, [those] who were and who will be until the consummation of the world. And yet again, likewise as we said, the seven candlesticks are the one and true Church in the seven-day week of the world that constitutes it, strengthened in faith in the Trinity, and confirmed in the sacrament of the celestial mysteries.

‘Certainly, concerning the angels, the question is turned around, if it be not unlocked fully.’

‘He said the stars were the angels of the churches, and the candlesticks the churches.’  Indeed, that is well known to ecclesiastical men.

There is not seven, but ‘one septiform church’ — that is, the complete, perfect one, as it is written: ‘“One is my dove, one is my perfect one.” [Songs 6:8]‘

‘But if it is one, there would not be seven angels.

‘But if they were seven, only those churches which are particularly named would have angels, and the rest not really have them.’

What will be sought besides is that these angels themselves each may want to have been assigned to the office of our angels; as the Truth is testified in the Gospel: “Their angels always see the face of My Father Who is in heaven.” [Mt. 18:10]

If these angels are assigned and joined to us, protecting us from all attacks and serving us, why couldn’t the Apostle Peter’s own angel pluck him out [of prison], and why was another sent to free him? For it says, “Now I know in truth that the Lord hath sent his angel, and hath delivered me out of the hand of Herod.” [Acts 12:11] And not only was the Angel sent, but he went back again, as it is written, and both “passed on through one street, and immediately the angel departed from him.” [Acts 12:10]

But some say, Each angel who is assigned is sent to free us. Therefore one might also say, If all the Apostles would have been in prison, twelve angels would have been sent — not another stranger’s angel, but his own would free him. For is not one smallest angel of the Lord able to strike the camp of the Assyrians, so as to free the whole of Jerusalem? [2 Kings/4 Reg. 19:35; 2 Chron. 32:21; Sirach 48:24] Or the holy angel with Job (and not the same one pointed out as the devil), was he not ordered to have endured something broader? [Job 33:28, Job 4:15, Job 5:1]

But if not protecting and freeing us, but strongly teaching us or instructing those given them, what is the Holy Spirit, of Whom the Lord says that when He comes, “He will teach you all things”? [John 14:26/John 16:13]

Still, should we say here that the Lord praised the little angels of His little ones? Does that mean younger humans have younger angels? And in what way? Did He say their angels merited to see the Lord? Were they not said to be given the keeping of some human? For if the angels see the Lord from their merit, it is not that the Lord mentions this in praise of the little ones. On the contrary, if our angels see God for our merits, did they not see God before they were assigned humans? Or do they examine a man to receive such angels. who may see and have seen God always?

I pass over in silence that they are unequal in merit; one can indeed, without danger of being contradicted, say that He may accept each angel for the mode of their faith. And as great as one will be, he will have so great an angel seeing God more worthily or more basically. Truly, as much less that they have been in devotion, so much less an angel have they deserved according to the merit of their devotion.

What do they do if holiness or faith grows, or a defect of the just diminishes? Do they remain, or are they changed by increases or decreases according to the faith of a man? For if angels are not changed, is either the angel of the man increasing the fruit of ascending in holiness thirty or a hundredfold, led with him, or left behind on the first step of progress?

But if to say this is puerile and vulgar, nor to be held in the divine Scriptures, but permitted to be understood by us, it is asked by us why he raised the least of their kind for praise, saying, “See that you despise not one of these little ones; for I say to you, that their angels in heaven always see the face of my Father who is in heaven.” [Mt. 18:10] For the higher could praise the lower, as in another place He praised them, saying, “As long as you did it to one of these the least of My brethren, you did it to Me.” [Mt. 25:40] For it is not better or higher praise of a man to have a guardian angel who always may see God, than to have Christ as a brother Who has him in His keeping always, and to have Christ deem him worthy to be His brother and co-heir. In this way, each one has an angel.

Is his angel’s voice his voice, or does the angel who is assigned to a man, have a voice, so that it was said, when Peter’s voice was heard, ‘That is not Peter, but his angel’? [Acts 12:15]

It cannot be held, for so great a suspicion will harm us if it may give occasion.

But how it is seen by me is that the angel of a man is his soul — that is, the interior man — who always is contemplating God with a pure heart by faith. As the Lord says, [Luke 13:35] “You shall not see Me till the time come, when you shall say, ‘Blessed is He that cometh in the name of the Lord.’” That is, when you believe and “take up” My “cross and follow Me.” [Mt. 16:24, Mk. 8:34, Lk. 9:23] And to Philip he says, “He that seeth me, seeth the Father also.” [John 14:9] Therefore, when it says ‘the angel of a man, it means the same man.’  ‘So you may understand the Churches and the Angels of them to be one.’

Not only is it a mystery, but also it is tradition handed down to divide one thing into many subjects, so that we say men of the Church, when the same men would be the Church. Angels, stars, and candlesticks are the same people. This is all one Church, but according to the sacrament of the mysteries, they are divided one by one.

Thus the Lord says, “My soul is sorrowful even unto death.” [Mt. 26:38] Therefore, it is seen that the one is Him Who speaks, and the other the soul of Him Who speaks, when He may say of that same soul, “My soul is sorrowful,” and not say, “I am sorrowful.” Therefore, if it is custom to speak so, and thus it is a shared union, how much more the mystery of the sacraments and the things obscured by grace? So that what is said through the mystery — that is, the concealed thing — you may understand now allegorically.

Allegory is the signifying of whatever kind of thing, so that one thing echoes in words another thing that you may understand in the mysteries — that is, in hidden things — and spiritually.

As it is said by Isaiah: “Behold, I do new things, and now they shall spring forth, verily you shall know them: I will make a way in the wilderness, and rivers in the desert. The beast of the field shall glorify me, the dragons and the ostriches, the sirens and the sparrows’ daughters: because I have given waters in the wilderness, rivers in the desert, to give drink to My people, to My chosen.” [Isaiah 43:19-20 - Vetus Latina]

For what is the “wilderness” where God makes a way, or what is the dry place where He has promised to make rivers, but the nations in which He has made known the way of life through Christ, and the unfailing water of salvation has flowed forth? Yet what are the beasts that bless and glorify God? What are the sirens and the daughters of the sparrows? What kind of chosen people are drinking, but the one and the same which was named above, who have followed Christ by straight faith, of works of justice? So that as you discuss this through a mystery and understand it to be one, so you may believe the angel and the church to be one, as you call body and soul one man.

That is seen to be no question with the Greeks in this. For among us, it is doubtful for the angel of a church to be either nations or leaders. But among them, it is obvious, because he did not say “the angel of this church” but “the angel to this church” — that is, the Bishop of the city.

‘So it is seen to speak not so much of an angel and a church, as what the Angel may wish to explain’, saying, “To aggelo tes en Epheso ekklesias graphon.” [Rev. 2:1] Which in Latin is: “To the angel” — that is, to the church — that is, “unto Ephesus, write.” You can also discern what with us is also plural in number, so that you count “to the angels unto the churches that are in Asia, write”.

So still in the Psalms it speaks plurally for the one: “He hath set his tabernacle in the sun in them.” [Ps. 18:6/Ps. 19:4] What may be in the sun, it explains, saying “in them,” when the sun and the tabernacle may be one in them.

So also he makes a question for us; for also ‘in the beginning’  of the book it had said ‘not seven angels, but seven churches.’  For so it says, “John to the seven churches that are in Asia.” [Rev. 1:4] And the Lord said to him, “Write what you have seen in a book, and send it to the seven churches.” [Rev. 1:11] Yet afterward He orders him to write “to the angels”, so as to show the angels and the churches to be one. ‘For “angel” in Latin is said “nuntius” [envoy, messenger]‘; “Ecclesia” is “convocatio” (convocation, assembly), because she would call all her own to penitence. In the end, does He order ‘angels to do penitence’?  No, but he orders the Church.

‘But these seven candlesticks are one septiform candlestick.’

‘Of this, the Lord says in the Gospel, “Neither do men light a candle and put it under a bushel, but they put it upon a candlestick.” [Matt. 5:15] By the bushel basket, He speaks of temporal profit; but by the candle [lamp], the light of preaching is received.’  Surely the candlestick is the men He has named. ‘Therefore, to put the candle under the bushel is to hide the grace of preaching for temporal profit, which certainly none of’ the servants of God ‘do.’  For as we have said, the candlestick is whoever the man is. ‘For when the candle is put on top, then the care of preaching is put first.’

This is properly the angel and the candlestick — that is, the announcement of preaching and the man by whom it is announced. For we read in another place: the virtue is to be one, from which seven [branches] go out, as the Lord ordered Moses to make and put in the tabernacle, so that it would blaze always. [Ex. 27:20, Ex. 35:37]

‘But this candlestick of seven branches bore the image of the Holy Spirit, Whose septiform grace lights up all the Church in consistent unity of faith.’  Or this ‘candlestick’ we ‘understand to be Christ’, carrying ‘the seven churches, which’ reflect ‘the septiform splendor of the Holy Spirit.’ 

‘And there are snuffers for these candlesticks, which are called “tongs” in Isaiah. [Ex. 25:38, Is. 6:6] These are the two Testaments, by which sins are purged.’

‘But the candlestick is outside the veil of testimony that is spread out; it is ordered to blaze because, without any veil of the Old Testament, the truth, the Holy Spirit, already shines. Clearly the Lord ordered the oil that is taken from olive trees; the Holy Spirit displays His grace.’  For ‘they have peace [olive branches = an offer of peace] and mercy through the coming of the Savior, which… He has kindled in our hearts.’

Nicholas of Lyra
 
The mystery of the seven stars That is, the mystery signified through them, which is explained when it is said: The seven stars are the angels of the seven churches, that is, their bishops, as the angel who was sent interprets. This is the name of the Episcopal office, just as it says in Malachi, “For the lips of the priests shall guard knowledge, and the law they shall seek from his mouth. For the angel of the Lord Almighty, he is.(Mal. 2:7)” The bishop is the principal priest in a diocese, whence the highest priest is called the supreme instructor among the ministers of God. And the seven candlesticks Just as candlesticks uphold candles, so the doctors of the Church who are the candles of the Church should be upheld by the good deeds of the Church. They are called golden because this rule ought to be accomplished by charity, which is signified by gold. Just as gold excels all other metals, so charity exceeds all other gfts of God, “For the greatest of these is charity.(1Cor. 13)”

Haydock Bible
 
Angels. These are the seven bishops of the churches. Christ's having them in his right hand, shews the care he takes of his Church. (Calmet)
